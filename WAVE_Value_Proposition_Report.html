1. Executive Summary

Overview of the Topic

Regenerative coastal and ocean tourism represents a transformative approach to hospitality development in sensitive marine environments. Unlike conventional or sustainability-focused tourism — which aims to reduce harm — regenerative tourism actively improves baseline ecological and social conditions. This report assesses the value proposition for WAVE's regenerative tourism pillar within Saudi Arabia's Red Sea ecosystem, and defines the scope and rationale for a targeted Social Return on Investment (SROI) analysis.

Saudi Arabia's coastal hospitality sector is expanding rapidly, presenting both risk and opportunity: under business-as-usual conditions, tourism growth can accelerate reef degradation, community displacement, and ecological loss. Regenerative interventions offer a pathway to reverse this trajectory while building a compelling, evidence-based business case for hospitality operators.

Headline Value Proposition

Regenerative tourism interventions in coastal Saudi Arabia can simultaneously preserve long-term asset value, reduce operational and environmental risk, attract premium market segments, and generate measurable positive outcomes for local ecosystems and communities. This creates a compounding return that conventional tourism models are structurally unable to replicate.

Key Insights

  • Evidence supports monetizable value to the hospitality sector across coral restoration, mangrove rehabilitation, sustainable seafood procurement, and community co-management — each linked to tourism revenue, avoided costs, and long-term asset durability.

  • Regenerative interventions go beyond sustainability by improving baselines. For example, restored reefs increase diver willingness-to-pay, mangroves reduce flood damage, and sustainable fisheries secure a local value chain that builds authentic destination identity.

  • Intangible social values — including local identity, cultural stewardship, and pride of place — are necessary components of a complete impact picture, even if the estimated size of this value is unclear.

Defined SROI Scope (High-Level)

The SROI will focus on a representative portfolio of eight investable interventions voluntarily adoptable by hospitality sector operators in coastal Saudi Arabia. Analysis will draw on international case studies to estimate potential impact in the Red Sea context, with explicit attention to pathways linking ecological change to economic and social value. Public policies and enabling-only interventions (such as data monitoring and guest engagement) are excluded from the core SROI scope but will be included as part of the narrative.

Recommended SROI Focus Areas

Based on Ecotone's evidence review, the eight interventions recommended for inclusion in the projected SROI of regenerative tourism include:

  • Coral gardening and outplanting (direct reef restoration)

  • Mangrove restoration and native coastal habitat regeneration

  • Advanced wastewater treatment

  • Green stormwater infrastructure

  • Reef-positive seafood procurement with fisher co-management

  • Ghost gear recovery

  • Local community micro-enterprise incubation

  • Local workforce development programs tied to regenerative programs for hotel employees

Key Risks and Uncertainties

  • Long time horizons for coral and mangrove regeneration may result in lower near-term SROI ratios.

  • It will be unclear the extent to which qualitative social value complements and adds to the quantitative estimates.

  • Transferability of international evidence to the Red Sea context requires careful assumption-setting and sensitivity analysis.

  • Governance continuity risk: if hotel ownership changes, regenerative practices may be abandoned without appropriate institutional structures.

2. Topic Overview & Strategic Context

Topic Definition

Regenerative tourism is a practice that seeks to leave destinations in a better state than they were found, drawing inspiration from regenerative agriculture's emphasis on restoring ecosystems and enhancing biodiversity. More precisely, it is a process through which tourism sector stakeholders collectively exert care and guardianship for the improvement and enhancement of natural, human, and built elements, thereby enabling those elements to exceed their current survivability conditions.

Regenerative coastal tourism specifically focuses on ocean- and coastal-connected interventions. Examples include coral reef restoration, mangrove rehabilitation, sustainable seafood systems, reduction of marine debris, and the empowerment of coastal communities as custodians of natural heritage. In contrast to sustainable tourism (which targets harm reduction) and business-as-usual tourism (which generates ongoing ecological and social cost), regenerative tourism improves baseline conditions through direct intervention and systemic change.

Alignment with Saudi Vision 2030

Regenerative coastal tourism is strongly aligned with Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030, particularly its objectives to diversify the economy, expand the tourism sector, and protect national natural assets. Vision 2030 identifies tourism as a strategic growth sector, with a target of increasing its contribution to GDP and positioning Saudi Arabia as a global destination. At the same time, it emphasizes environmental sustainability, quality of life, and the preservation of cultural and natural heritage.

In this context, regenerative tourism offers a pathway to reconcile rapid tourism development with long-term ecosystem health. By embedding restoration, community participation, and natural capital enhancement into tourism models, regenerative approaches directly support Vision 2030 priorities related to sustainable economic diversification, environmental protection, and the creation of high-value, differentiated tourism offerings.

Strategic Relevance to WAVE

WAVE's regenerative tourism strategy is anchored in the Red Sea ecosystem of Saudi Arabia, one of the most biodiverse marine environments and thermally resilient coral ecosystems on the planet. The strategy aims to build a compelling business case for the hospitality sector to voluntarily adopt interventions that deliver measurable ecological and community benefit, creating a replicable model for nature-positive tourism development.

WAVE's focus areas span ocean ecosystem health (coral, mangroves, water quality), local community empowerment, green infrastructure, sustainable supply chains, and data monitoring innovations. This SROI work is designed to translate these interventions into rigorous, evidence-based value estimates that hospitality operators, investors, and policymakers can act on.

3. Intervention Landscape

Intervention Categories

Given the breadth of potential interventions associated with coastal tourism, a structured screening framework was used to determine which actions should be considered within the regenerative tourism portfolio. The first step was to distinguish between sustainable and regenerative interventions. Sustainable interventions are defined as those that reduce negative environmental or social impacts relative to business-as-usual conditions, but do not materially improve the baseline state present when the operator arrives. Regenerative interventions, by contrast, are those that create net-positive outcomes by restoring, enhancing, or strengthening ecological and social systems beyond their initial condition.

Regenerative interventions within the portfolio were further differentiated between direct and indirect pathways. Direct regenerative interventions are those that actively restore, rebuild, or enhance ecosystems or communities through the intervention itself. Examples include coral gardening and outplanting, mangrove restoration through hydrology-first approaches, ghost gear recovery with fisher participation, and coastal community micro-enterprise incubation linked to traditional boatbuilding and low-impact marine excursions.

Indirect regenerative interventions, by contrast, enable or accelerate regeneration by improving the conditions that allow ecosystems or communities to recover naturally or at scale. Examples include advanced wastewater treatment beyond compliance, green stormwater infrastructure, and desalination brine mitigation. While these measures may not restore habitats directly, they can remove pressures, improve water quality, and generate the information needed for directing investments.

Interventions were also assessed according to their scale of impact: some actions create benefits largely within the footprint of a single operator, while others generate benefits that extend beyond the operator boundary into surrounding ecosystems, communities, or destination economies. Using these three lenses — impact type, pathway, and scale — the long list of interventions was categorized into a practical typology (Table 2).

System Boundaries

To keep the analysis decision-useful and focused on what hospitality operators can realistically influence, the following boundaries were established:

Included:

  • Investable, clearly defined interventions voluntarily adoptable by hospitality sector operators

  • Ocean- and coastal-connected impact pathways (direct and indirect)

  • International evidence base applied to the Red Sea / Saudi Arabia context

  • Ecological, social, and economic value streams, including non-monetizable outcomes in the narrative

Excluded:

  • Broad sector-level sustainability claims and public policy interventions

  • Giga-project-scale interventions (e.g., NEOM, Red Sea Global) that are not replicable by typical operators

  • Enabling-only interventions (data monitoring, guest engagement) from the SROI calculation (though included in narrative)

  • Interventions without a credible evidence pathway to monetizable outcomes

Landscape Observations

The current landscape for regenerative coastal tourism remains relatively nascent, with visible activity concentrated among a limited number of advanced operators and destinations that combine premium tourism models with strong natural assets. Examples often referenced in the market include Soneva, Misool Eco Resort, and selected initiatives associated with Red Sea Global.

A key observation from the scoping process is that many interventions can be implemented at multiple scales. Coral gardening, reef-safe moorings, local procurement programs, or wastewater upgrades may operate at the level of a single hotel, a cluster of resorts, or an entire destination. Scale matters because broader deployment can generate stronger ecosystem outcomes, shared infrastructure efficiencies, and more coherent destination branding than isolated property-level implementation.

4. Evidence Review & Key Insights

Summary of Evidence

The evidence base for regenerative tourism is strongest where ecological improvement can be linked directly to visitor demand, avoided operating risk, or established ecosystem-service values. The most mature evidence clusters around coral-reef-dependent tourism, mangrove ecosystem services, nature-based coastal protection, and consumer willingness to pay for environmentally differentiated hospitality products.

The macroeconomic context reinforces the importance of the topic. Travel and tourism contributed just over $9.9 trillion to the global economy in 2023, representing 9.1% of global GDP (World Travel & Tourism Council, 2024). Coastal and marine tourism also represents a major share of the sector, with World Bank materials estimating that it accounts for nearly half of global tourism activity (World Bank, 2025).

At the same time, the evidence is uneven across intervention types. Ecological and economic evidence is relatively strong for coral reefs, mangroves, and visible destination-quality interventions, but weaker for intangible social outcomes such as stewardship, cultural continuity, local pride, and community identity.

What Drives Impact

Several factors distinguish higher-impact regenerative interventions from weaker ones. First, the strongest interventions improve shared destination assets, not only the environmental performance of individual properties. In marine tourism, visitor experience and long-term destination competitiveness depend on the condition of shared reefs, habitats, fisheries, beaches, and coastal systems.

Second, community co-management and local stewardship are often central to durability. This is especially true for fisheries, coastal habitat restoration, visitor management, and marine conservation, where behavior, enforcement, and long-term stewardship shape ecological outcomes alongside capital investment.

Third, the clearest monetizable pathways are those in which ecological quality is visibly linked to tourism demand, visitor experience, or avoided loss. Reef quality, marine habitat condition, beach quality, and other visible environmental attributes can affect recreational value, destination choice, and willingness to pay.

Fourth, scaling matters. Hotel-level pilots can generate proof of concept, but many of the strongest pathways in regenerative tourism operate at the level of shared ecosystems and shared destination assets rather than within a single property.

Evidence Strength and Gaps

Strong evidence exists for coral-reef-related tourism value. Spalding et al. (2017) estimated the global annual value of coral reef tourism and demonstrated that reef-related tourism value is spatially concentrated, making destination-specific analysis especially important. There is also strong evidence for mangrove ecosystem-service valuation, particularly for coastal protection, carbon, habitat support, and other regulating services.

Evidence is moderate to strong for environmentally differentiated hospitality value. A systematic review by Damigos and Kontogianni (2023) found that consumers who are willing to choose greener hotels may accept a price premium, while also noting substantial variation by geography, traveler segment, and hotel type.

The evidence becomes weaker in three areas: monetization of intangible social value; geographic transferability to the Red Sea context; and the combined SROI of multiple regenerative interventions implemented simultaneously.

Key Insights

What works. The clearest monetizable pathways connect ecological condition directly to tourism demand, visitor experience, or avoided loss. Coral reef quality is the strongest example. Mangroves and nature-based coastal interventions are also promising because they can generate multiple categories of value at once.

Limitations and trade-offs. Time horizon is a major issue. Coral and mangrove recovery often unfold over years or decades. SROI design should handle discounting carefully and complement monetized estimates with narrative treatment of longer-term ecological and social value.

Scaling and implementation. Pilot interventions at the asset level can generate useful early evidence, but the largest value creation is likely to occur when interventions shift the ecological and reputational trajectory of the destination as a whole.

5. Value Creation Across the Topic

Core Investment Value Pathways

The scoping analysis identified three overarching investment pillars that structure value creation in regenerative coastal tourism. These pillars are reinforcing: asset durability supports revenue, risk mitigation protects that revenue, and capital efficiency improves the returns available to investors who fund the transition.

Pillar 1: Asset Durability & Revenue Preservation

Conventional coastal tourism erodes its own foundations: reef degradation reduces willingness-to-pay, brand damage creates volatile demand, and rising remediation costs impair asset value over time. Regenerative tourism reverses this trajectory.

  • Avoided reef degradation costs (restoration costs avoided through prevention)

  • Sustained fisheries productivity supporting local supply chains and authentic experiences

  • Coastal protection value from mangroves and reef structures (wave attenuation, storm damage avoided)

  • Higher willingness-to-pay from certified regenerative experiences

  • Longer average stays and repeat visitation from engaged, value-driven guests

  • Reduced seasonality volatility through diversified nature-based offerings

Pillar 2: Risk Mitigation & Downside Protection

Conventional tourism operators face mounting risks from regulatory tightening, loss of social license, and climate vulnerability. Regenerative operators are structurally better positioned.

  • Policy alignment reduces regulatory shock exposure

  • Community buy-in reduces operational disruption risk

  • Greater climate resilience through natural infrastructure investments

  • Flood incidence and severity reduced (avoided infrastructure costs)

  • Reduced operational risks from environmental degradation

Pillar 3: Capital Efficiency & Financing Advantage

Regenerative operators can access capital structures unavailable to conventional operators, improving overall returns and reducing cost of capital.

  • Unlocks patient capital from impact investors and blended finance instruments

  • Attracts strategic NGO, government, and research partnerships that offset costs

  • Demonstrates value created per dollar invested (SROI > IRR as communication tool)

  • Shows leverage of non-commercial capital alongside private investment

Tourism as a Value Aggregator

A defining feature of regenerative coastal tourism is its capacity to bundle multiple ecosystem improvements into monetizable experiences. Coral restoration, mangrove rehabilitation, ghost gear removal, and sustainable seafood sourcing each create discrete ecological improvements, but their combined effect generates a differentiated destination identity that commands premium pricing and attracts a distinct, growing market segment of values-based travelers.

This bundling effect means that the aggregate SROI of a regenerative tourism portfolio exceeds the sum of individual intervention SROIs. The destination becomes a product in itself, and the ecological health that underpins it is the asset being invested in.

Summary of Selected Intervention Types

Intervention 1: Coral Gardening and Outplanting

Description: Cultivating coral fragments in nurseries and strategically outplanting them to degraded reef areas. Can be implemented at the hotel level through partnerships with marine biologists or through shared industry programs.

Rationale: Strong international evidence base; directly aligns with WAVE's reef ecosystem focus; creates a clear link between ecological restoration and hospitality operator value through reef quality and marine tourism demand.

Intervention 2: Mangrove Restoration with Coastal Habitat Regeneration

Description: Hydrology-first restoration of mangrove systems combined with community stewardship programs. Designed to restore ecological function while supporting habitat connectivity, nutrient filtration, coastal resilience, and local engagement.

Rationale: Mature evidence base across multiple coastal markets; generates several value streams at once including coastal protection, carbon storage, habitat quality, and destination resilience.

Intervention 3: Advanced Wastewater Treatment Beyond Compliance

Description: Wastewater treatment systems that exceed minimum regulatory standards, including nutrient polishing, tertiary treatment, water reuse, and discharge practices designed to protect sensitive coastal ecosystems.

Rationale: Strategically important in arid coastal destinations where water quality is material to ecosystem health. Clear links to reef resilience, water clarity, avoided ecosystem degradation, and destination reputation.

Intervention 4: Green Stormwater Infrastructure

Description: Nature-based and hybrid infrastructure solutions including bioswales, permeable surfaces, constructed wetlands, restored wadis, retention landscapes, and vegetated drainage systems.

Rationale: Creates dual value through climate adaptation and marine protection. Particularly relevant where episodic rainfall events generate flash runoff and sediment discharge that can affect coastal water quality and reef health.

Intervention 5: Reef-Positive Seafood Procurement with Fisher Co-Management

Description: Hotel purchasing programs that prioritize locally caught and sustainably managed seafood, combined with support for fisher participation in reef monitoring and co-management schemes.

Rationale: Links hospitality value chains directly to community livelihoods and marine ecosystem health. Addresses the cultural heritage dimension prioritized by WAVE by supporting local fisheries and place-based food systems.

Intervention 6: Local Workforce Development for Regenerative Practices

Description: Training and certification programs for local residents and hospitality workers in regenerative tourism practices including reef restoration, sustainable guiding, environmental monitoring, and waste recovery.

Rationale: Connects ecological investment with employment creation, social inclusion, and long-term operational capacity. Particularly relevant to national workforce development and localization agendas in Saudi Arabia.

Intervention 7: Coastal Community Micro-Enterprise Incubator

Description: Targeted incubation and market-linkage support for locally owned coastal enterprises focused on traditional boatbuilding, heritage maritime skills, and low-impact marine excursions.

Rationale: Directly reflects WAVE's interest in linking regenerative tourism with community participation and cultural heritage. Creates a practical mechanism for distributing tourism revenues beyond resort boundaries.

Intervention 8: Ghost Gear Recovery with Community Recycling and Upcycling

Description: Organized recovery of abandoned, lost, or discarded fishing gear from reef and coastal habitats, combined with pay-for-return incentives, community collection efforts, and downstream recycling or upcycling.

Rationale: An emerging but compelling circular economy model with strong alignment to ocean health, community income generation, and WAVE's blue economy priorities.

Value Distribution

Value flows through a reinforcing cycle across stakeholder groups. Hospitality operators that invest in reef restoration, mangrove regeneration, water quality, local procurement, and community partnerships can improve destination quality while differentiating their offering. These improvements may attract higher-value guests, strengthen willingness to pay, support repeat visitation, and create revenue that can be reinvested into further regenerative activity.

Ecological improvements also create value beyond the operator. Healthier reefs, mangroves, and coastal systems can support fisheries, reduce pollution and erosion risk, strengthen shoreline resilience, and improve the quality of guest experiences. Local communities, fishers, and producers may benefit through employment, enterprise development, sustainable procurement, and greater participation in tourism value chains.

Public value is created through avoided costs, stronger natural capital, expanded local economic activity, and potential contributions to national diversification and sustainability goals. The SROI analysis will place particular emphasis on operator-to-ecosystem-to-operator pathways because these often provide the clearest and most commercially attributable monetization opportunities.

Key Causal Pathways

The logic model includes three primary causal architecture types, each with distinct monetization mechanisms:

  • Ecological restoration → tourism demand pathway: Improved reef or mangrove condition increases habitat quality and visitor experience, which increases willingness-to-pay and visitation volumes, translating into operator revenue.

  • Ecological protection → avoided cost pathway: Restored natural infrastructure reduces damage from floods, sedimentation, and storm events, generating value through avoided remediation and maintenance costs.

  • Community empowerment → supply chain value pathway: Local workforce and community participation in regenerative practices reduces operator HR costs, strengthens authentic destination identity, and creates sustainable local supply chains.

9. SROI Design Foundations

Intervention 1: Coral Gardening and Outplanting

Measurement Focus

The SROI should assess whether coral gardening improves reef condition, increases destination quality and visitor experience, and translates into commercial value for hospitality operators through enhanced willingness to pay, occupancy, or positioning. The monetized case may include avoided reef degradation costs, revenue uplift from improved guest experience, and the value of restored ecosystem services.

Key Metrics & Indicators

Metric / Indicator Relevance to SROI
Coral cover (% change) Primary indicator of ecological recovery; links to destination quality.
Reef structural complexity Indicator of habitat recovery; affects fish populations and dive experience quality.
Diver willingness to pay Revenue pathway; measures consumer value of reef condition.
Average daily room rate (ADR) Revenue pathway; tracks premium positioning associated with reef quality.
Avoided restoration costs ($/ha) Avoided cost pathway; measures long-term cost savings from preventive restoration.

Critical Assumptions and Uncertainties

  • Rate of coral establishment is dependent on water temperature, bleaching risk, and physical disturbance — all elevated in the Red Sea context.

  • WTP transfer from other reef markets requires adjustment for Red Sea visitor demographics and destination maturity.

  • Long recovery time horizons (10–20 years) require careful discounting assumptions.

Cross-Cutting SROI Design Considerations

Across the intervention portfolio, several assumptions will strongly influence SROI results. First, ecological benefits often accrue over long time horizons, while many operator costs occur upfront. Discount rates, recovery curves, and benefit timing should be tested through sensitivity analysis.

Second, several benefits depend on transferring evidence from other geographies. These transfer assumptions should be clearly documented and adjusted for the Red Sea context.

Third, the analysis should distinguish between benefits that can be monetized with reasonable confidence and benefits that should be treated narratively. Operator revenue, cost savings, employment income, carbon value, and avoided damage costs are more likely to support monetization. Cultural identity, stewardship, community trust, destination authenticity, and long-term sense of ownership may be highly material but should likely be treated through qualitative evidence.

Finally, the SROI should avoid implying false precision. The most useful output may be a scenario-based model that shows low, base, and high cases for each intervention, supported by transparent assumptions.

10. Realizing the Business Case: Implementation, Governance, and Scale

The business case for regenerative tourism is strong, but its realization depends on several important strategic considerations related to implementation, scale, and system dynamics.

First, regenerative interventions are rarely implemented in isolation — and the portfolio effect matters.

While most available evidence assesses interventions individually, in practice they are typically deployed together, and there is strong rationale for deliberate bundling. Complementary interventions can both reduce ongoing damage and accelerate recovery simultaneously: reef-safe moorings implemented alongside coral restoration efforts are a clear example, eliminating a source of physical disturbance while active replanting is underway. The combined and synergistic effects of bundled interventions remain an area where evidence is still developing, but the directionality is clear — well-sequenced portfolios are likely to outperform the sum of their parts.

Second, the feasibility of specific interventions is often highly context-dependent.

Certain solutions are only viable in particular geographic or environmental conditions along the Red Sea coastline. Technologies such as seawater air conditioning (SWAC) may only be feasible in locations with appropriate bathymetric and thermal characteristics, such as parts of the Gulf of Aqaba or near Jeddah. Similarly, ecosystem-based interventions depend on the presence and condition of underlying habitats.

Third, governance and incentive structures are critical to sustaining regenerative outcomes over time.

Hospitality assets may change ownership or management within relatively short time horizons, creating a risk that regenerative practices are not maintained. This highlights the importance of complementary mechanisms — destination-level standards, certification systems, long-term stewardship agreements, or policy frameworks — to ensure continuity and protect investments in natural capital.

Fourth, regenerative interventions often operate over long time horizons.

Ecological recovery processes — particularly for coral reefs and coastal ecosystems — may take years or decades to materialize. This temporal dynamic can affect how certain interventions appear when evaluated using standard discounting approaches, even where long-term value creation is substantial. Framing these as infrastructure-class investments, rather than short-cycle operational returns, is important for attracting the right capital and setting appropriate expectations.

Finally, achieving meaningful scale will require a diversified portfolio of interventions deployed across different geographies and systems.

Scaling potential varies significantly: some interventions may demonstrate strong returns but apply only in limited contexts, while others are more broadly replicable across the hospitality sector. Certain natural systems — reef-based tourism in particular — are also subject to ecological carrying capacity constraints that limit the volume of visitors that can be sustainably accommodated. These dynamics reinforce the case for portfolio diversification as a scaling strategy, and position regenerative tourism not as a single replicable product, but as a destination-shaping approach that compounds in value as more operators participate.

11. Strategic Implications & Business Case

Strategic Importance for WAVE

This SROI analysis positions WAVE as an evidence-based advocate for regenerative coastal tourism in the Red Sea context while also clarifying the business case for operator adoption and investment. By translating ecological and social outcomes into commercially relevant value, the analysis can help move regenerative tourism from aspiration to investable strategy. It can also support several strategic purposes: encouraging hospitality operators to voluntarily adopt regenerative interventions, attracting impact investors and blended finance, informing destination standards, and positioning Saudi Arabia as a global reference point for nature-positive tourism.

The strategic timing is favorable. Tourism remains one of the world's largest economic sectors, contributing just over $9.9 trillion to the global economy in 2023, or 9.1% of global GDP (World Travel & Tourism Council, 2024). At the same time, tourism can place significant pressure on the ecological systems that make destinations valuable, with global tourism estimated to account for approximately 8% of global greenhouse gas emissions (Lenzen et al., 2018). This dual role — as both a driver of environmental pressure and a beneficiary of healthy ecosystems — creates a strong case for regenerative models that go beyond harm reduction.

For premium coastal destinations, coral reefs, mangroves, water quality, marine biodiversity, and cultural heritage are not peripheral amenities — they are core components of the tourism product. Coral reef tourism alone has been estimated at nearly $36 billion annually worldwide (Spalding et al., 2017). In the Red Sea context, regenerative investment can therefore be framed not only as environmental stewardship, but as a strategy for protecting and enhancing the destination assets that support pricing power, visitor satisfaction, repeat visitation, and long-term competitiveness.

The business case for regenerative tourism rests on several mutually reinforcing pathways: ecological restoration and protection that improves nature-based visitor experiences; nature-based and infrastructure interventions that reduce costs by avoiding damage and degradation; visible regenerative leadership that strengthens brand equity and attracts values-aligned travelers; improved ecological resilience and governance that reduces climate, reputational, and regulatory risks; and credible evidence of impact that improves access to values-aligned capital.

Regenerative tourism also aligns with broader policy and financing priorities. The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework calls for mobilizing at least $200 billion per year for biodiversity by 2030 (Convention on Biological Diversity, 2022). Tourism is well positioned to contribute to this agenda because it is a major beneficiary of ecosystem services, particularly in coastal and marine destinations.

Key Takeaways

  • Regenerative tourism in the Red Sea context has a multidimensional value proposition. It can improve ecological conditions, strengthen community participation, reduce risk, support premium positioning, and enhance long-term hospitality asset value.

  • The distinction between sustainable and regenerative tourism is strategically important. Sustainable tourism often focuses on reducing harm, while regenerative tourism focuses on improving the underlying ecological, social, and cultural assets that make destinations valuable.

  • The business case is strongest where regenerative interventions can be linked to revenue growth, cost avoidance, risk reduction, brand differentiation, asset resilience, and access to values-aligned capital.

  • A complete SROI should monetize the clearest value pathways while also recognizing material non-monetized outcomes, including cultural identity, local stewardship, pride of place, community trust, and destination authenticity.

12. Next Steps

Phase 2 will move from value proposition development and scope definition to rigorous quantification of the selected SROI components. Building on the intervention portfolio, stakeholder mapping, evidence review, and logic model developed in Phase 1, the next phase will generate monetized impact estimates, SROI ratios, and sensitivity analyses for prioritized regenerative tourism pathways.

The immediate priority is to convert the current strategic scope into a practical modeling plan. This will require finalizing the intervention portfolio, collecting site- and operator-specific data, identifying monetization methods, building the SROI model, and reviewing assumptions with WAVE and relevant implementation partners.

1. Finalize the SROI Scope

The first step is to confirm which interventions should be included in the Phase 2 SROI model. This should include reviewing the selected intervention portfolio with the WAVE team and determining whether any interventions should be added, removed, combined, or reframed based on stakeholder feedback and implementation feasibility.

This step should also clarify whether the SROI will model each intervention separately, model a bundled portfolio, or use both approaches.

2. Collect Priority Data for Monetization

Phase 2 should prioritize data collection for the pathways most likely to support credible monetization.

Data Area Examples of Data Needed

3. Build the SROI Model and Document Assumptions

The SROI model should estimate monetized benefits, costs, SROI ratios, and stakeholder-level value distribution for the prioritized pathways. As the model is built, key assumptions should be documented transparently, including assumptions related to ecological recovery rates, visitor willingness to pay, operator adoption costs, discount rates, carbon prices, avoided-cost estimates, and the transferability of evidence from other regions.

Because many assumptions will carry uncertainty, the model should include low, base, and high scenarios. Sensitivity analysis should be used to show which assumptions have the greatest influence on results.

4. Review Assumptions and Preliminary Results with Stakeholders

Once the initial model has been developed, assumptions and preliminary results should be reviewed with WAVE, Red Sea Global, hospitality operators, marine research partners, and relevant community or supply-chain stakeholders. This review should focus on whether the assumptions are realistic, whether the monetization methods are credible, and whether the results align with how regenerative tourism could actually be implemented in the Red Sea context.

5. Finalize the SROI Outputs

The final Phase 2 outputs should include an evidence-based, decision-ready SROI analysis that communicates not only whether regenerative tourism creates value, but where, for whom, over what time horizon, and under what implementation conditions. Outputs should include monetized impact estimates, SROI ratios, sensitivity analysis, stakeholder-level value distribution, and a clear distinction between monetized, quantified-only, and narrative outcomes.

B. Key Definitions

Glossary of Terms

Term Definition
Sustainable tourism Tourism that reduces negative environmental and social impacts without improving baseline conditions. Defined as harm reduction.
Regenerative tourism Tourism that actively improves environmental or social conditions beyond the baseline present at the start of the intervention. Focuses on leaving destinations better than found.
Direct regenerative intervention Activities that actively restore, revive, or enhance ecosystems or communities through direct action (e.g., coral gardening, mangrove planting).
Indirect regenerative intervention Activities that enable or accelerate regeneration by improving conditions that allow systems to recover naturally (e.g., mooring buoy installation to reduce anchor damage).
Within-footprint intervention Impact confined to the physical or operational boundary of the hospitality developer. Consistent with sustainability; not regenerative.
Beyond-footprint / systemic intervention Impact that extends beyond the boundary of the operator and creates positive change in surrounding systems. The defining characteristic of regenerative impact.
SROI (Social Return on Investment) A framework for measuring and accounting for the social, environmental, and economic value generated by an investment. Expressed as a ratio of value created per unit of investment.
Enabling intervention Activities that support or facilitate direct interventions but do not independently generate ocean or community impact (e.g., data monitoring, guest engagement programs).
Monetize (in this context) To assign a monetary value to a social or environmental outcome — not to generate revenue from it. Includes willingness-to-pay estimates, avoided cost calculations, and ecosystem service market values.
